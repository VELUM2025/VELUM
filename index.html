Mobile robots are increasingly required for exploration, inspection, and mapping in confined or hazardous environments. Multirotor drones are the most widely used aerial platform because of their agility and hover capability, but their flight time is severely constrained by battery capacity, typically limited to less than one hour. In addition, rotor downwash can disturb fragile surfaces, and vibration compromises the performance of onboard sensors.

Small lighter-than-air (SLTA) vehicles provide a viable alternative. By relying on buoyant lift instead of rotors, SLTAs can remain airborne for days, carry heavier payloads relative to their size, and offer a naturally stable platform with minimal power consumption. These features make them well suited for extended missions in narrow passages, humid conditions, and safety-critical environments where endurance and low interference are essential.

Despite these advantages, SLTA performance is constrained by the envelope material. Commercial systems typically rely on polyurethane (PU) or biaxially oriented polyester (Mylar®). PU is flexible and durable but heavy and permeable to helium, reducing useful lift and operational time. Mylar has low permeability but is brittle and prone to catastrophic tearing, complicating handling and limiting its reliability. Both options impose design trade-offs that restrict autonomy in practice.

VELUM – Vehicle Envelope with Lightweight Ultrafilm for Minimal Leakage – addresses this gap by introducing a low-density polyethylene (LDPE) film reinforced with a specialized coating. LDPE provides lower mass and high tear tolerance, while the coating substantially reduces helium leakage without compromising flexibility or thermal bondability. This combination yields a lighter, stronger, and more reliable envelope material tailored to SLTA requirements.

The main contributions of this work are:

  • Identification of coated LDPE as a superior envelope material that balances low density, tear resistance, and gas-barrier performance better than PU and Mylar®.
  • Demonstration that coating treatment reduces helium leakage with only marginal weight increase, preserving the lightweight advantage of LDPE for SLTA design.
  • Development of a fabrication and coating protocol compatible with thermal bonding, ensuring manufacturability of large, airtight membranes.
  • Validation of the material and design in real confined and humid field environments, proving feasibility for long-duration SLTA operation beyond laboratory testing.

Manufacturing Process

The envelope is fabricated by cutting precision-shaped membrane panels (“gores”) from LDPE film and thermally bonding them to form the body. Sealing is performed with a heat roller that applies direct heat and pressure under controlled conditions, allowing continuous, uniform joints that accommodate curved or unconventional geometries and sharp edges (areas where other sealing methods often fail). The process uses a non-stick barrier and heat-resistant silicone mat to protect the film, while custom fixtures maintain edge alignment for complex shapes. This method produces airtight, durable seams without adhesives, making it suitable for both simple and highly irregular airship designs.

Coating

To improve helium retention and surface durability, the LDPE envelope is coated after all seams are sealed, since the coating layer eliminates the film’s thermal-bonding capability. The process is performed while the envelope is inflated (“under-load”) to avoid uncoated voids from later stretching. A fluoro-siloxane sol-gel coating is applied using the optimal spray-and-wipe method, which combines high-pressure spraying with microfiber wiping for maximum uniformity and adhesion. This yields a smooth, defect-free surface with minimal cracks or cavities, outperforming spraying or wiping alone.

Procedure:

Surface Preparation: The inflated envelope is secured in a rigid carbon-tube frame to prevent deformation. Dust and contaminants are removed with an alcohol-based cleaner sprayed at 5–6 bar from 500 mm distance, followed by microfiber wiping.

Spraying: Two even spray passes at constant distance to control coating thickness and weight.

Wiping: Microfiber cloth dampened with coating liquid is used in a single direction within 10–15 minutes before gelation.

Curing: 48 hours at 25 ± 1 °C with ventilation. The surface becomes slightly cream-colored and rougher, indicating morphological changes that enhance barrier performance.

Performance Gains:

Helium leakage resistance is improved due to reduced surface defects and voids. Hydrophobicity increases significantly, with the coated LDPE showing the highest contact angle (117.53°), improving water repellency as well. Coating remains intact under repeated deformation, maintaining barrier integrity in handling and deployment.

Tests and Results

The coated LDPE envelope was benchmarked against polyurethane (PU) and biaxially oriented polyester (Mylar®) through density, mechanical, and helium leakage evaluations. Results show that LDPE maintains a clear weight advantage, with only a small mass increase after coating, remaining lighter than PU while offering greater flexibility than Mylar®. In standardized tensile, tear, puncture, and burst tests, coated LDPE provided a balanced profile: more durable than Mylar® yet significantly lighter and less permeable than PU. Gas barrier measurements confirmed that the applied coating substantially reduced helium permeability, yielding the lowest leakage-to-mass ratio among the tested materials. A comparative scoring framework integrating all tests ranked coated LDPE highest overall, establishing it as the most suitable choice for lightweight, durable, and low-leakage SLTA envelopes.

Gas-barrier evaluation for full-scale SLTA envelopes does not adhere to any specific ASTM standard. Conventional gas-leakage setups (e.g., standard leak detection chambers) are designed for small, flat test samples and cannot accommodate the large, complex geometries of LTA envelopes. Moreover, no existing detectors can directly and accurately measure helium leakage at the scale of full airship membranes.

  • Lack of applicable ASTM standard: Existing standards such as ASTM D1434—used for measuring gas permeability of plastic films—are limited to small coupon samples and cannot be applied to entire envelopes.
  • Inadequacy of typical fixtures: Diffusion chambers and permeability testers are physically too small for even moderate-size airship envelopes, making them unsuitable for practical evaluation.
  • Special methods required for large envelopes: Industry often relies on differential-pressure, sweep-gas, “sweep-and-hold,” or “accumulation-and-purge” methods to assess helium permeability in airbags or large modules, rather than relying on small film coupons.
  • Practical implications: Our approach uses a full-envelope system test, capturing leakage behavior under realistic conditions rather than relying on small-scale proxies.

Custom pillow-shaped samples (800 × 1200 mm) were fabricated from LDPE, PU, and Mylar films. Each sample was thermally sealed and inflated to 4 kPa with helium, then suspended from a 50 N load cell to monitor lift over time. The lift was recorded continuously at 1.5-minute intervals for six hours in a sealed, temperature-controlled room (26–27 °C). Initial stabilization (first 10 minutes) was excluded.

This setup captures the combined effects of film permeability, seam quality, and geometry, making it more representative of real operational envelopes than small-scale diffusion tests. Durability was also assessed by subjecting each deflated sample to tumble agitation (30 minutes, no heat, with dryer balls), then repeating the helium retention protocol.

Unlike the other tests (tear, puncture, tensile, and wettability) that followed ASTM standards, the burst test was performed using a protocol commonly adopted in the LTA industry for envelope evaluation. Pillow-shaped samples (800 × 1200 mm) were sealed and inflated with compressed air until rupture, while internal pressure was continuously recorded. This approach replicates the biaxial stress state experienced by inflated envelopes and highlights each material’s failure behavior.

  • Maximum Pressure Capacity: Mylar reached the highest pressure (3.53 kPa) but failed explosively at the seam transition, reflecting its brittle nature. PU failed at the lowest pressure (1.11 kPa) after prolonged plastic deformation and leakage. LDPE withstood 3.08 kPa before rupture in the bulk film, indicating strong seals and a balanced strength–ductility profile.
  • Failure Mode: Mylar fragmented into multiple sharp tears, PU underwent ductile thinning and gradual tearing, while LDPE failed in a controlled, localized slit without catastrophic propagation.
  • Practical Implication: The LDPE membrane showed safer rupture behavior and reliable seam integrity, an advantage for reusable small indoor airships where uncontrolled failure would compromise missions.

Deployment

To evaluate VELUM’s real-world feasibility, a deployment trial was carried out in a partially flooded cave characterized by narrow passages, high humidity, and fragile surfaces that preclude direct human access. These conditions replicate the type of confined, high-risk environments where small lighter-than-air (SLTA) vehicles provide a unique operational advantage over conventional drones.

The coated low-density polyethylene (LDPE) envelope was selected based on its superior balance of lightweight construction, gas retention, and durability, as established in laboratory evaluations. Despite the addition of a heavier valve assembly (20.74 g, including a 7 g cap), the total envelope mass remained significantly lower than that of a comparable polyurethane (PU) design, confirming its suitability for constrained endurance-critical missions.

During the cave deployment, the coated LDPE airship demonstrated stable flight, minimal helium loss, and resilience under humid conditions. The lightweight membrane reduced structural load while maintaining envelope integrity, allowing reliable operation in passages where maneuverability and persistence are critical. These results validate the coated LDPE envelope as a practical solution for autonomous airship missions in confined and hazardous environments.
