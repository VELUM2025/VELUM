Introduction

This page complements a journal paper (submitted) on VELUM, a lightweight coated-LDPE membrane for small lighter-than-air (SLTA) vehicles. It provides extra visuals and brief highlights only.

Conventional envelopes—PU (durable but heavy and more permeable) and Mylar® (light and tight but brittle)—force trade-offs. Our coated LDPE approach targets low mass, better tear tolerance, and lower leakage while keeping simple, scalable fabrication.

Manufacturing Process

The envelope is fabricated by cutting precision-shaped membrane panels (“gores”) from LDPE film and thermally bonding them to form the body. Sealing is performed with a heat roller that applies direct heat and pressure under controlled conditions, allowing continuous, uniform joints that accommodate curved or unconventional geometries and sharp edges (areas where other sealing methods often fail). The process uses a non-stick barrier and heat-resistant silicone mat to protect the film, while custom fixtures maintain edge alignment for complex shapes. This method produces airtight, durable seams without adhesives, making it suitable for both simple and highly irregular airship designs.

Coating

To improve helium retention and surface durability, the LDPE envelope is coated after all seams are sealed, since the coating layer eliminates the film’s thermal-bonding capability. The process is performed while the envelope is inflated (“under-load”) to avoid uncoated voids from later stretching. A fluoro-siloxane sol-gel coating is applied using the optimal spray-and-wipe method, which combines high-pressure spraying with microfiber wiping for maximum uniformity and adhesion. This yields a smooth, defect-free surface with minimal cracks or cavities, outperforming spraying or wiping alone.

Procedure:

Surface Preparation: The inflated envelope is secured in a rigid carbon-tube frame to prevent deformation. Dust and contaminants are removed with an alcohol-based cleaner sprayed at 5–6 bar from 500 mm distance, followed by microfiber wiping.

Spraying: Two even spray passes at constant distance to control coating thickness and weight.

Wiping: Microfiber cloth dampened with coating liquid is used in a single direction within 10–15 minutes before gelation.

Curing: 48 hours at 25 ± 1 °C with ventilation. The surface becomes slightly cream-colored and rougher, indicating morphological changes that enhance barrier performance.

Performance Gains:

Helium leakage resistance is improved due to reduced surface defects and voids. Hydrophobicity increases significantly, with the coated LDPE showing the highest contact angle (117.53°), improving water repellency as well. Coating remains intact under repeated deformation, maintaining barrier integrity in handling and deployment.

Tests & Results

Only a short overview is shown here — full data and methods are detailed in the submitted paper.

  • Weight: Coated LDPE remained lighter than PU, with only a minor mass increase after coating.
  • Durability: Tear resistance higher than Mylar; seam strength remained reliable under stress.
  • Leakage: Lowest leakage-to-mass ratio among all three materials tested.
  • Hydrophobicity: Highest water-repellency (contact angle >117°).
  • Burst test: LDPE ruptured in a controlled slit (safe failure), PU failed at lower pressure, Mylar failed explosively.

Visuals below illustrate representative leakage and burst behaviors.

Gas-barrier testing required custom methods, as no ASTM standards apply at full-envelope scale. Pillow samples (800 × 1200 mm) were helium-inflated to 4 kPa and suspended from a 50 N load cell in a controlled room (26–27 °C). Lift was monitored over six hours to capture permeability, seam quality, and geometry effects. Durability was assessed by tumble agitation before repeating retention tests.

Burst tests (800 × 1200 mm samples, compressed air) replicated biaxial stress conditions. Mylar reached the highest pressure but failed explosively, PU failed at the lowest pressure after ductile thinning, and LDPE ruptured at 3.08 kPa in a controlled slit, showing strong seals and safer failure behavior. This confirms LDPE’s balanced strength–ductility profile and reliable seam integrity for reusable airships.

Deployment

To validate VELUM’s real-world feasibility, we conducted a trial in a partially flooded cave with narrow passages, high humidity, and fragile surfaces where direct human access is restricted—exactly the kind of confined, high-risk environment where small lighter-than-air (SLTA) vehicles excel over conventional drones.

The coated LDPE envelope—selected for its balance of low mass, durability, and helium retention—outperformed polyurethane (PU) in full-scale use. Even with a heavier valve assembly (20.74 g, including a 7 g cap), the LDPE envelope weighed only 343 g versus PU’s 401 g, preserving payload capacity for a lidar and micro-camera. Field results showed stable flight, superior helium retention, moisture resistance, faster deployment, and longer endurance. The LDPE vehicle supported a 250 g payload (vs. 164 g for PU), remained inflated for days without refilling (PU required multiple refills per day), deployed in ~15 min (vs. 25 min), and sustained 72 h of operation (vs. 24 h). Together, these outcomes validate coated LDPE as a robust, lightweight, and resilient material for autonomous airship missions in confined and humid environments.
